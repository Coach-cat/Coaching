Coaching & Confidence

Coaching, Performance, and the Pursuit of Excellence

Long before I became a career coach, resume writer, and interview strategist, I was a high performance athlete and coach.

Over the years, I coached athletes in alpine ski racing, track and field, endurance sports, and Ironman triathlon. I also competed at the highest levels of endurance sport, representing my country at World Championship events across four different distances, from Olympic distance triathlon to the Ironman World Championship in Kona, Hawaii.

Through both coaching and competition, I learned something that applies just as much to careers as it does to sports:

Performance is performance.

Whether you're standing on a start line, presenting to senior leadership, interviewing for a dream job, or navigating a challenging job search, the same principles apply.

Success requires preparation.
Success requires consistency.
Success requires the ability to perform under pressure.
And success requires believing in yourself, especially on the days when confidence feels hard to find.

Athletes quickly learn that progress is rarely linear. There are great training days and difficult training days. There are personal bests and setbacks. There are moments when motivation is high and moments when it disappears completely.

The same is true during a job search.

Some days you'll receive interview requests. Other days you'll hear nothing. Some opportunities will move forward. Others won't. The candidates who ultimately succeed are not always the most talented. They are often the ones who continue showing up, refining their approach, and moving forward when others quit.

One of the most important lessons I learned as both an athlete and a coach is that mindset matters.

If you begin a race convinced you will fail, you've already placed a ceiling on your performance.

The same is true in your career.

You must walk into an interview believing you belong in the room. You must communicate your value with confidence. You must trust your preparation and be willing to perform when the opportunity presents itself.

My role as a coach is not simply to help you write a stronger resume or prepare better interview answers.

My role is to help you build the confidence, clarity, and mindset necessary to perform at your best when it matters most.

Because when preparation meets belief, remarkable things become possible.

And when you consistently show up, do the work, and trust the process, success often follows.

Strategic Career Writing

Is Your Resume Ready?

Your resume is one of the most important marketing documents of your career.

A recruiter may spend 5 to 30 seconds reviewing it. An ATS or AI screening tool may spend even less than a second.

The question is simple: Is your resume making the right impression?

Your resume should clearly demonstrate that you have the skills, experience, and achievements required for the role. It should quickly answer the question every hiring manager is asking:

"Why should I spend time interviewing this person?"

Does your resume showcase your accomplishments, or does it simply list responsibilities and job duties?

Is it easy to read, or are there large blocks of text, inconsistent formatting, and dense paragraphs that make recruiters work harder than they should?

Have you packed in every role you've ever held, including your high school job from 20 years ago, because you were a "Manager" at a mall kiosk?

Do certain words appear so often that they lose all meaning? (If "increased sales," "team player," or "strategic leader" show up every few lines, you may have a case of résumé déjà vu.)

Today's AI and ATS systems are becoming increasingly sophisticated. They can identify repetition, vague language, inconsistencies, missing context, and formatting issues faster than ever before. They can also recognize when a resume lacks focus, clarity, or relevance to the target role.

The reality is that resume expectations have changed.

Fancy graphics, excessive design elements, and complicated layouts may look impressive, but if they confuse recruiters or ATS systems, they can work against you. Your resume should be clear, focused, and easy to navigate.

You can still present yourself professionally with a clean, modern design. Save the deeper career stories, personality, and executive summaries for interviews and networking conversations.

Ask Yourself These Questions

  • Would my resume perform well in an ATS screening?
  • Does every bullet point demonstrate action, impact, and value?
  • Is the content results focused rather than task focused?
  • Is the document easy to scan in 10 seconds?
  • Have I removed unnecessary repetition, buzzwords, and filler language?
  • Are there gaps, inconsistencies, or unclear career transitions that might raise questions?
  • Does this resume clearly differentiate me from other candidates?
  • Would a recruiter remember me after reviewing dozens of similar resumes?

Most importantly: does this resume make someone want to call me?

Positioning & Interview Prep

Interviewing Is a Performance: Are You Ready for the Spotlight?

Most candidates spend hours updating their resume and LinkedIn profile, yet surprisingly little time preparing for the interview itself.

The interview is where hiring decisions are made. It is your opportunity to demonstrate not only that you can do the job, but that you can communicate your value with confidence, clarity, and credibility.

After coaching hundreds of professionals, I consistently see three mistakes that prevent otherwise qualified candidates from landing offers.

Mistake #1: Talking About "We" Instead of "I"

Many candidates answer questions by describing what their team, department, or organization accomplished.

The interviewer is not hiring your team.

They are hiring you.

If you don't clearly explain your individual contributions, the interviewer is left wondering:

  • What was your specific role?
  • What decisions did you make?
  • What problems did you solve?
  • What impact did you personally create?

Strong candidates are comfortable using the word "I."

Not because they are arrogant, but because they understand that interviews are designed to assess individual performance.

Mistake #2: Walking Into an Interview Without Proven Stories

Most interview questions are simply different ways of asking:

"Tell me about a time when..."

Yet many candidates try to think of examples on the spot.

The result is rambling answers, forgotten details, and missed opportunities.

The strongest candidates prepare five to six powerful STAR stories before the interview.

These stories should demonstrate:

  • Leadership
  • Problem solving
  • Conflict resolution
  • Innovation
  • Adaptability
  • Results and impact

When prepared correctly, a handful of strong examples can answer the majority of behavioral interview questions.

Preparation creates confidence. Confidence creates credibility.

Mistake #3: Having an Elevator Pitch That Sounds Like a Resume

One of the most important questions in any interview is often the first:

"Tell me about yourself."

Unfortunately, many candidates respond by reciting their work history, education, and every position they have held over the last twenty years.

The interviewer can already see that information on your resume.

Instead, your elevator pitch should be a concise one to two minute introduction that highlights:

  • Your greatest strengths
  • The value you bring to organizations
  • Why you are an excellent fit for the role
  • What differentiates you from other candidates

Most importantly, include a brief example of a significant accomplishment that demonstrates your impact.

Don't tell the entire story.

Create curiosity.

Give just enough information to make the interviewer want to ask:

"Tell me more about that."

The best elevator pitches act as a trailer, not the entire movie.

One Final Thought

Great interviews are rarely won by the most qualified candidate.

They are often won by the candidate who communicates their value most effectively.

Avoid filler words. Eliminate long pauses. Organize your thoughts. Practice your stories. Refine your pitch.

When preparation meets opportunity, confidence follows.

And confidence is memorable.
